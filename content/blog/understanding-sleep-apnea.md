Sleep apnea is one of the most common sleep disorders affecting adults in the United States, with an estimated 22 million Americans suffering from the condition. Despite its prevalence, approximately 80% of moderate to severe cases remain undiagnosed. Understanding this condition is the first step toward getting the treatment you need.

## What is Sleep Apnea?

Sleep apnea is a potentially serious sleep disorder characterized by repeated interruptions in breathing during sleep. These breathing pauses, called apneas, can last from a few seconds to over a minute and may occur hundreds of times per night.

There are three main types of sleep apnea:

### Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

The most common form, OSA occurs when the throat muscles intermittently relax and block the airway during sleep. This is often accompanied by loud snoring and gasping sounds as the body struggles to resume breathing.

### Central Sleep Apnea

This less common type occurs when the brain doesn't send proper signals to the muscles that control breathing. It's often associated with underlying medical conditions affecting the brainstem.

### Complex Sleep Apnea Syndrome

Also known as treatment-emergent central sleep apnea, this condition occurs when someone has both obstructive and central sleep apnea.

## Recognizing the Warning Signs

Many people with sleep apnea don't realize they have a problem because the symptoms occur during sleep. Common signs to watch for include:

- **Loud, chronic snoring** - Often the first symptom noticed by a bed partner
- **Episodes of stopped breathing** - Witnessed by another person
- **Gasping or choking during sleep** - Your body's response to resume breathing
- **Morning headaches** - Caused by changes in oxygen levels
- **Excessive daytime sleepiness** - Despite seemingly adequate sleep
- **Difficulty concentrating** - Related to poor sleep quality
- **Irritability and mood changes** - Sleep deprivation affects emotional regulation
- **Dry mouth or sore throat upon waking** - From breathing through the mouth

## Risk Factors

While anyone can develop sleep apnea, certain factors increase your risk:

- **Excess weight** - Fat deposits around the upper airway can obstruct breathing
- **Neck circumference** - People with thicker necks may have narrower airways
- **Age** - Sleep apnea occurs more frequently in older adults
- **Gender** - Men are two to three times more likely to have sleep apnea
- **Family history** - Having family members with sleep apnea increases your risk
- **Use of alcohol or sedatives** - These substances relax throat muscles
- **Smoking** - Smokers are three times more likely to have OSA
- **Nasal congestion** - Difficulty breathing through your nose increases risk

## The Importance of Diagnosis

Untreated sleep apnea can have serious health consequences, including:

- High blood pressure and heart problems
- Type 2 diabetes
- Liver problems
- Complications with medications and surgery
- Sleep-deprived partners

A sleep study, also called polysomnography, is the gold standard for diagnosing sleep apnea. This test monitors your brain activity, eye movements, heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen levels, and breathing patterns while you sleep.

## Treatment Options

The good news is that sleep apnea is highly treatable. Treatment options include:

### Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP)

CPAP therapy is the most common and effective treatment for moderate to severe sleep apnea. A CPAP machine delivers a constant stream of air pressure through a mask, keeping your airway open during sleep.

### Oral Appliances

For mild to moderate sleep apnea, dental devices that reposition the jaw and tongue can help keep the airway open. These are custom-fitted by a dentist.

### Lifestyle Changes

Weight loss, regular exercise, avoiding alcohol before bed, and sleeping on your side rather than your back can significantly improve symptoms.

### Surgery

In some cases, surgical procedures to remove tissue, reposition the jaw, or implant nerve stimulators may be recommended.

## Taking the Next Step

If you suspect you or a loved one may have sleep apnea, don't wait to seek help. Contact a sleep specialist or visit an accredited sleep center for evaluation. With proper diagnosis and treatment, you can dramatically improve your sleep quality and overall health.
